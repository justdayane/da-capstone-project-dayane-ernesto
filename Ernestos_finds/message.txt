Here's a comprehensive data collection framework for your hypothesis.

Recommended study period: 2005 – 2024. This window captures Gen Z entering drinking age (roughly 2015+) while giving you a solid baseline of Millennial and Gen X consumption behavior in their equivalent youth years for a fair age-matched comparison — the most important methodological choice here.

Core Indicators to Collect
Primary consumption metrics

Per capita alcohol consumption in liters of pure alcohol (LPA), broken down by age group
Drinking prevalence (% of population who drinks) by age cohort
Heavy episodic drinking / binge drinking rates by age group (a critical metric, as abstinence and binging can coexist)
Frequency of drinking (daily, weekly, monthly, never) by age group
Age of drinking initiation trends over time

Beverage type breakdown

Beer, wine, spirits, and RTD (ready-to-drink) share by age group — Gen Z is known to shift toward low/no-alcohol alternatives, and this granularity will reveal it

Contextual & explanatory variables

Alcohol pricing and affordability index (real price over time)
Minimum legal drinking age per country
Alcohol advertising restrictions timeline per country
Cannabis legalization status (a potential substitution effect)
Mental health indicators by cohort (anxiety, depression rates — linked to sober-curious movement)
Social media penetration rates by year (a hypothesized behavioral driver)
GDP per capita and youth unemployment rates (economic stress vs. leisure spending)


Data Sources
Pan-European & WHO sources (start here)

WHO Global Status Report on Alcohol and Health — who.int — provides LPA, prevalence, and heavy drinking rates by country and some age breakdowns
ESPAD (European School Survey Project on Alcohol and Other Drugs) — espad.org — your best source for youth data; surveyed 15–16 year olds across 35+ European countries every 4 years since 1995
Eurobarometer surveys — ec.europa.eu/commfrontoffice/publicopinion — attitudinal and behavioral surveys including alcohol, with age group breakdowns
EHIS (European Health Interview Survey) — Eurostat — household-level survey with alcohol consumption frequency by age, sex, and country

National health & statistics agencies

UK: NHS Digital / OHID "Health Survey for England" and "Smoking, Drinking and Drug Use among Young People"
Germany: Bundeszentrale für gesundheitliche Aufklärung (BZgA) — youth drug affinity surveys
France: Santé publique France — Baromètre santé
Nordic countries: NOMESCO database, and each national institute of public health

Commercial & industry data (for triangulation)

IWSR Drinks Market Analysis — tracks beverage volume by category and has youth market reports (some free summaries)
Euromonitor / Statista — paid, but university access is common; excellent for beverage-type splits


Methodological Notes to Keep in Mind

Age-match your cohorts. Don't compare Gen Z at age 20 today to Millennials at age 35 today. Compare Gen Z at age 20 to Millennials when they were 20. This is the most common analytical error in generational studies.
Control for COVID-19 (2020–2021). Drinking patterns spiked and then shifted dramatically — treat these as outlier years or include a dummy variable if you're running regression.
Distinguish abstinence from moderation. A generation can show lower average consumption while having unchanged drinking-occasion intensity. ESPAD and EHIS both let you separate these.
Country clustering. Europe is not homogeneous — consider grouping into Northern (high spirits, beer), Southern (wine-dominant), and Eastern Europe, as generational trends differ significantly across clusters.
Panel vs. cross-sectional. ESPAD is your best shot at a repeated cross-sectional design, which is the closest to a longitudinal signal you'll get from public data.
